Periodic Table Explorer
 
Welcome!
 

The Periodic Table Explorer is split in to six main sections.
 
Data
 
By default the elements are listed by name, in alphabetical order. Clicking the left-hand button on the top toolbar gives access to: Name, Atomic Number, Boiling point, Melting point, Discovery date, Electronegativity (Pauling), Atomic Volume, Atomic Radius, Abundance (Sun, Universe and Earth's Crust), Enthalpy of Atomisation, Enthalpy of Fusion, Enthalpy of Vapourisation, Heat Capacity, Thermal Conductivity, Thermal Expansion, Speed of Sound and Valence Electron Potential.
 
Clicking an element will hightlight it (press the speech bubble icon on the top toolbar to hear the element name), click the element again to cause the list to rotate, showing the element's details on the reverse, in the information browser.
 
Information browser
 
The information browser. Select from different types of information via the buttons on the bottom toolbar;
 
Main data, physical properties, reactions and production/isolation.
 
Click the ... (more) button for access to Atomic Reference, Compounds, Isotopes, Mass Attenuation Data and Spectra.
 
Clicking a hyperlink on any page will can take you to biographies (cyan), element pages (green), glossary (blue) and country (red). The browser knows which element is currently being displayed so clicking a link to Lithium and then selecting Spectra from the "More..." menu will open the Spectra page for Lithium.
 
Use the arrows on the right of the toolbar to navigate through the viewed pages, they work in the same way as your favourite web browser.
 
Click the "Back" button to return to the element list.
 
Click the "photo" icon to see the images for the selected element.
 
Images
 
View a set of images for the selected element, images for elements Berkelium and later are not available!
 
Drag left and right for access to the element's images.
 
Cycle through the elements by using the up and down arrows at the right of the top toolbar.
 
Click the "Back" button to return to the information browser.
 
Periodic Table
 
Click the button to access a Periodic Table. Click any element to see the following properties:
 
Name, Symbol, Group, State at 298K, Colour, Atomic Number, Mass (g/mol), Boiling Point (K), Melting Point (K) and Electronegativity (Pauling) and absorption spectrum (top of the display).
 
Click the "graph" icon to highlight elements based on one of the following properties:
 
Density, Electronegativity, Melting Point, Boiling Point, Atomic Radius, Atomic Volume, Valence Electron Potential, Enthalpy of Atomisation, Enthalpy of Fusion, Enthalpy of Vapourisation, Thermal Conductivity and Thermal Expansion.
 
The elements are coloured according to property value. Low values are purple, moving through blue, cyan, green, yellow, orange and the highest values in red.
 
Click the button to select from several highlight modes:
 Elements that are solid at 298K
 Elements that are liquid at 298K
 Elements that are gas at 298K
 Elements that are synthetic
 Elements that are radioactive
 
Click the button beneath the graph icon to select either absorption or emission spectra (A and E respectively).
 
Click the speech bubble icon to hear the English pronunciation of the element's name.
 
Reference
 
Quick access to biographies, glossary of terms and country production data.
Use the arrows on the right of the toolbar to navigate through the viewed pages, they work in the same way as your favourite web browser.
 
Toolbox
 
Click the search icon on the left of the top toolbar to change mode.
 
Find compounds, equations, physical and natural constants and search through all of the Periodic Table Explorer's 1400+ pages of content.
 
Use the arrows on the right of the toolbar to navigate through the viewed pages, they work in the same way as your favourite web browser.
 
Calculate the molecular weight of any compound. Typing H2O will calculate the formula for water.
 
Complicated formulas are also supported, such as C55H72O5N4Mg, as are those with brackets (KCl2)3.
 
It's important to note that to calculate a formula such as NaCl.2H2O use NaCl(H2O)2 instead.
